01 — Executive Framing

Why this analysis now

The pharmacovigilance outsourcing market is undergoing a structural shift. This is not a trend to monitor — it is already a competitive reality.

The EMA's 2026 GVP update has made AI-powered pharmacovigilance tools and automated signal detection systems an expectation, not an option for MAHs. Simultaneously, the EMA–FDA joint Good AI Practice Principles published in January 2026 have moved AI in PV from "interesting pilot" to regulated activity. The EU AI Act's high-risk rules are taking effect in August 2026, and any AI system deployed in a PV workflow will carry formal validation, documentation, and human oversight obligations.

This means one specific thing for Biomapas: the window to position proactively — before clients require it contractually — is approximately 12–18 months. Competitors are already moving. ArisGlobal presented at the 40th Pharmacovigilance UK & EU conference in March 2026 on "Agentic AI and the Next Horizon for Pharmacovigilance" — explicitly framing the shift from task automation toward decision intelligence. Parexel acquired Vitrana and its AI-powered PV platform in early 2026. AstraZeneca acquired Boston-based AI partner Modella AI to bring foundation models in-house.

Biomapas must decide now: build, buy, partner, or fall behind. This document maps the operational reality of the PV vertical, identifies where automation and AI create real value, assesses the vendor/partner landscape relevant to Biomapas's geography and client profile, and makes a case for a sequenced strategic response.

02 — Operational Baseline

What the PV vertical actually is

Before any automation or AI discussion is credible, the process architecture must be understood precisely. The PV vertical is not a single service — it is a modular stack of at least nine distinct operational layers.

2.1 The nine operational layers

The critical insight is that ICSR Case Processing and Literature Screening together represent the highest-volume, most-labor-intensive, and most time-critical operations — and are therefore simultaneously the highest-cost layers and the highest-automation opportunity layers. Any AI investment that does not address these two layers first is strategically misaligned.

2.2 Data ownership: the most underestimated constraint

Biomapas operates as a GDPR Data Processor, not a Data Controller, across all PV operations. The MAH (client) owns the data. This has three non-negotiable consequences for any AI strategy:

  • Biomapas cannot use client ICSR data to train a proprietary AI model without explicit contractual permission from each MAH client. This is a legal constraint, not a technical one. It is also a commercial negotiation point for every new client agreement from 2026 onward.
  • Every AI tool Biomapas integrates into a validated GxP workflow (Veeva Vault Safety) must itself be validated under Computer System Validation (CSV) requirements and documented accordingly. Adding AI without a validated CSV package exposes the client MAH to regulatory inspection risk — which is the MAH's problem, but also Biomapas's reputational and contractual risk.
  • Signal detection visibility gaps exist in markets where national authority forwarding is the only data channel — particularly relevant in markets outside the EU/UK where no EudraVigilance-equivalent query access exists. This is an operational risk that requires proactive NCA relationship management, not a technology solution.

2.3 Geographic reality check

The document maps a broad geographic footprint, and this is Biomapas's documented market positioning. However, for strategic purposes — particularly when evaluating automation ROI, vendor partnerships, and M&A targets — active revenue markets must be distinguished from aspirational or exploratory ones.

Core active markets where Biomapas operates with strength: Lithuania and the Baltics, Poland, Romania, Bulgaria, Czech Republic, Slovakia, Hungary, France, Belgium, Netherlands, UK, and Turkey. These are the markets where Biomapas has established client relationships, active QPPV network coverage, and recurring revenue — and they represent the EU-27 and UK regulatory environment that accounts for the substantial majority of operational volume.

Markets on the horizon: Certain CIS/EAEU markets (Armenia, Kazakhstan, Georgia, and others in the region) are being explored and some clients exist there, but they represent a smaller portion of current activity and should be treated as a longer-term geographic expansion layer — not as a primary focus for near-term AI or automation investment decisions.

Markets being watched: Japan, Singapore, China, and other APAC markets may be relevant in a longer-term growth scenario but are not material to the current operational or automation discussion.

Any AI or automation investment must be sequenced to deliver ROI in the core EU and UK markets first. Building a validated AI intake pipeline optimized for EudraVigilance E2B R3 submissions, MHRA Yellow Card gateway automation, and TİTCK EBS integration creates immediate value across the largest client base. Extending capability into other markets can follow once the core infrastructure is proven and scalable.

03 — AI & Automation Application

Where AI and automation can be applied: an honest assessment

The process mapping reveals a clear tiered structure of automation readiness. This is not a list of aspirations — it is a prioritized investment map.

Tier 1

High readiness, high value, act now

ICSR Data Extraction OCR + NLP

The single highest-impact, fastest-deployable automation. Incoming reports arrive as unstructured PDFs, emails, phone call transcripts, and web forms across all EU markets. Manually re-keying data from these into Veeva Vault Safety is labor-intensive and error-prone. AI document extraction using OCR and NLP is already embedded in Veeva Vault Safety's native Case Intake Agent. The question for Biomapas is not whether to use this — it is whether it is currently activated and properly configured for all client workflows, including multilingual inputs from key EU markets (Polish, Romanian, French, Dutch, Turkish). If this is not fully deployed, it is the first thing to fix.

MedDRA Auto-Coding

MedDRA coding is among the most resource-intensive sub-tasks in case processing. An experienced coder spends significant time per case; dictionary version updates create re-coding cycles. AI-assisted auto-coding with mandatory human confirmation is technically mature, with TransCelerate's industry initiative already benchmarking accuracy. Vendors including ArisGlobal NavaX and Oracle Argus AI modules offer this capability today. The critical prerequisite: the active MedDRA version in the safety database must be locked and systematically updated before any AI coding layer is added.

Expectedness and Reportability Assessment Rule Engine + NLP

Comparing verbatim event terms against the current SmPC/CCDS for expectedness, and then applying multi-market reportability logic (EU 15-day rule, MHRA 15-day rule, TUFAM 15-day rule) is a rule-heavy task that follows explicit decision trees. This is highly suitable for rule-engine automation augmented by NLP for label comparison. For Biomapas operating across multiple EU member states plus UK and Turkey simultaneously, a validated multi-market reportability engine reduces the single largest risk of deadline breach — and a >99% on-time expedited submission rate is the industry benchmark that client contracts are built around.

Literature Screening PubMed/Embase + NLP Triage

Weekly search execution across PubMed and Embase APIs is already technically automatable — both databases offer programmatic access. The high-value AI layer is NLP-based abstract triage: classifying retrieved articles as "clearly irrelevant," "requires full-text review," or "contains potential ICSR." This step currently consumes significant human attention and can be substantially accelerated with a trained NLP classifier.

A note of competitive context is warranted here. Local language literature screening across EU markets — Polish, Romanian, Czech, Slovak, Lithuanian, French, Dutch, Turkish — is not fully automatable with off-the-shelf tools, because many regional journals are not comprehensively indexed in standard APIs. Biomapas's native-language specialist capability in this area is operationally valuable and difficult to replicate at speed. However, the competitive moat should not be overstated: large global CROs such as IQVIA and Parexel maintain local specialist networks across major EU markets and are actively investing in multilingual AI tooling. Notably, ArisGlobal launched NavaX Translation in February 2026 — a certified, pharma-grade multilingual translation layer embedded directly into LifeSphere Safety workflows — signalling that technology-driven multilingual capability is advancing rapidly.

Biomapas's advantage in this area is real but time-limited and geography-specific: it is strongest in CEE markets (Polish, Romanian, Czech, Slovak, Lithuanian) where global CRO local presence is thinner, and weaker as a differentiator in Western European markets. This capability should be preserved, not automated away, and positioned explicitly to clients as a compliance service — particularly for CEE-heavy product portfolios — while recognising that it will require ongoing investment to remain competitive as multilingual AI tooling matures.

PSUR/PBRER Data Compilation Automated Extraction + Table Generation

Data lock point extraction from Veeva Vault Safety, case count tables by SOC and seriousness, interval summary statistics — these are structured data extraction tasks that are fully automatable from a validated database. Several vendors now offer report shell pre-population directly from safety database queries. This reduces PSUR preparation time materially and shifts human effort toward the benefit-risk narrative and QPPV review, where it belongs.

Submission Compliance Tracking Real-Time Dashboard

Veeva Vault Safety already has partial dashboard functionality. A fully configured real-time compliance dashboard — tracking every pending ICSR by jurisdiction, deadline, and current processing stage — is a low-cost, high-value operational tool that both reduces internal risk and creates a visible compliance demonstration for client audits. If not fully configured today, this is a quick win.

Tier 2

Medium readiness, requires investment and governance decisions

LLM-Based Narrative Writing Draft + Medical Review

Large language model drafting of ICSR narratives is technically available and already piloted by several CROs. The regulatory constraint is important: a medically qualified person must review and sign off before submission. This does not prevent AI drafting — it constrains the workflow to AI-draft, human-review-and-approve. The risk is quality control: LLM narratives may be fluent but clinically imprecise, and the downstream liability if a poorly drafted narrative contributes to a missed signal is real. This is a medium-term investment requiring careful validation, not an immediate deployment.

Signal Detection Enhancement NLP + Emerging Sources

Quantitative signal detection (ROR, PRR, EBGM disproportionality analysis) is already semi-automated in Veeva Vault Safety's signal module. The AI enhancement opportunity is extending signal detection to unstructured text sources: narrative text from ICSRs, published literature flagged in screening, and — for clients with social media monitoring obligations — digital channels. Agentic AI systems described in the March 2026 ArisGlobal presentation explicitly target this layer, describing the move from "task automation to decision intelligence". This is a 2–3 year investment horizon for Biomapas, not an immediate deployment.

Tier 3

Structural limitations or governance prerequisites required

PSMF Dynamic Automation, SOP Version Management, CSV Documentation Generation

All of these are medium-readiness automation opportunities that require process-explicit SOPs as a prerequisite. If any PV processes at Biomapas are currently managed by experienced staff relying on tacit knowledge rather than fully documented decision logic, automation cannot proceed until that documentation is complete. This is an internal readiness question that must be answered before vendor evaluations begin.

04 — Regulatory Tailwind

GVP 2026 as a commercial accelerator

The most strategically significant external development is the EMA's 2026 GVP update, which makes AI-powered PV tools and automated signal detection systems an expectation, not an option for MAHs. The EMA has confirmed that GVP modules will be revised per ICH M14 (real-world data and evidence in post-market safety assessment), and companies adopting AI must demonstrate documented human oversight and validation traceability.

This creates an immediate commercial dynamic for Biomapas: clients who currently lack AI-integrated PV systems now face a regulatory compliance gap. A Biomapas that can offer validated, AI-augmented PV services — with documented human oversight and GVP-compliant audit trails — becomes the solution to that gap rather than a passive observer of it.

Simultaneously, the EU AI Act's classification of many healthcare AI applications as high-risk means that any AI tool deployed in a regulated PV workflow carries formal validation obligations. This is a barrier to entry for smaller or less technically sophisticated competitors — and a reason why a well-structured AI integration at Biomapas, done properly with full CSV validation, is defensible and difficult to replicate quickly.

The January 2026 EMA–FDA joint Good AI Practice Principles further signal that regulatory harmonization on AI in PV is accelerating. Companies that are already compliant with these principles when auditors begin inspecting AI tools — likely 2027 onwards — will face dramatically lower compliance burden than those still in implementation.

06 — Decision Framework

Build vs. buy vs. partner

The €200M revenue target by 2030 requires that Biomapas's PV vertical grows substantially — and AI/automation is both a margin lever and a revenue expansion lever. The options are not mutually exclusive, but they require different capital allocation and different timelines.

Option A · Immediate · Low Cost

Native Platform Maximization

What: Fully activate all existing AI/automation features in Veeva Vault Safety (Case Intake Agent, signal detection module, E2B automation, dashboard configuration). Develop process-explicit SOPs as automation prerequisites. Configure multi-market reportability rule engines for EU/UK/Turkey.

Why: Lowest-cost, fastest-ROI action available. Most CROs running Veeva Vault Safety are not using 100% of available features. The investment is in internal capability building and configuration, not in new software.

What it does not solve: Differentiated AI capabilities beyond the Veeva roadmap; local language literature screening automation for broader EU markets; custom signal analytics. This option is a foundation step, not a complete AI strategy. The gap between Veeva's current native AI and dedicated AI-first platforms such as ArisGlobal NavaX is real and commercially relevant — Veeva's roadmap does not guarantee parity on the 12–24 month timeline that matters for competitive positioning. Option A buys time; it does not replace Options B and C.

A Note on the "Build" Option

Why pure proprietary build is not recommended now

A fully proprietary AI build — developing Biomapas's own NLP models, training pipelines, and validated AI infrastructure from the ground up — is not recommended as a primary strategy at this stage, and this assessment is made deliberately rather than by omission.

The core reasons are structural. Biomapas does not currently have a dedicated data science function or AI engineering team. Recruiting and retaining this talent in the Lithuanian and Baltic market is possible but time-consuming and increasingly competitive, particularly as the broader EU tech sector continues to absorb available AI talent. Even with accelerated hiring, a credible internal AI build — one that meets GxP validation standards, performs reliably on multilingual ICSR data, and covers the full EU regulatory submission landscape — would require a minimum of 3–4 years to reach production-ready deployment. That timeline is not compatible with the 12–18 month competitive window identified in Section 1.

This does not mean internal capability is irrelevant. In the 24–48 month horizon, as Biomapas's AI integration matures through Options B and C, building selective internal AI competency — particularly for CEE-language NLP tasks where off-the-shelf tools underperform — becomes both feasible and strategically valuable. The sequencing matters: integrate and partner first, build selectively once the operational and data foundations are in place.

07 — Compliance Layer

The AI integration compliance layer: not optional

A point that must be explicit to the Biomapas Leadership: AI integration in PV is not purely a technology decision — it is a regulatory compliance and legal decision. Three non-negotiable requirements apply:

  1. CSV Validation for every AI component Any AI tool added to a GxP-validated system (Veeva Vault Safety) must itself be validated under EU Annex 11 / GAMP 5. This means IQ/OQ/PQ protocols, validation master plan updates, and change control documentation. Biomapas must budget for this before any AI tool goes live in a client-facing workflow.
  2. Human-in-the-loop for all expedited submissions Under current GVP (and the 2026 update), a medically qualified person must review and approve every expedited ICSR before submission. No AI tool replaces this step. AI accelerates the upstream process; the human approval gate is non-negotiable and must be preserved in any workflow redesign.
  3. DPA amendments for AI data usage If Biomapas plans to use any ICSR data — even anonymized — to train or fine-tune AI models, this requires explicit consent in updated Data Processing Agreements with every MAH client. This is both a legal requirement and a commercial negotiation point. New client contracts should include AI data usage permissions as a standard clause from 2026 onward.
08 — Internal Readiness

Key questions Biomapas Leadership must answer internally

The following cannot be resolved from external analysis. They are operational and contractual facts that block automation design if unanswered.

  1. Veeva Vault Architecture Is Biomapas running one shared multi-tenant Vault instance for all clients, or separate Vault instances per MAH? Multi-tenant architecture limits cross-client automation. This is the first architectural question to answer.
  2. AI Feature Utilization Audit Is the Veeva Vault Safety Case Intake Agent (AI document extraction) currently activated and configured for all clients? If not, this is an immediate unrealized value gap.
  3. ICSR Volume Mapping What is the monthly ICSR volume per client, per market? Automation ROI is entirely volume-dependent. A client processing 5 cases/month has different automation economics than a client processing 200 cases/month.
  4. DPA Status Do all existing MAH client contracts have GDPR-compliant DPAs that address AI data usage? If not, they must be updated before any AI tool that touches ICSR data is deployed.
  5. SOP Explicitness Are all PV SOPs written with explicit inputs, decision logic, outputs, and responsible roles — or are some steps dependent on experienced staff tacit knowledge? Process-explicit SOPs are the technical prerequisite for RPA and AI-assisted workflows. This audit should precede any vendor evaluation.
  6. EudraVigilance Credential Structure Are EVDAS credentials held at company level or at individual QPPV level? If individual-level, QPPV staff changes create EV access disruption — a dependency risk for automated signal detection workflows.
  7. Multi-Market Submission Routing Logic For clients with products authorized across multiple EU member states, UK, and Turkey simultaneously — is the per-market submission routing logic fully documented in SOPs, or is it held as tacit knowledge by senior staff? This is the most complex automation challenge in the entire PV system and must be documented before any submission automation can proceed.
09 — Revenue Connection

Why this matters for the €200M target

The connection between AI/automation in the PV vertical and the €200M target by 2030 is direct, but it operates through three separate mechanisms — not one.

Mechanism 1

Margin expansion through efficiency

ICSR processing is the highest-cost, most labor-intensive operation. If AI tools (document extraction, auto-coding, rule-based reportability) reduce processing time per case by 40% — a benchmark cited in multiple 2025–2026 industry analyses and already reported by ArisGlobal NavaX users — the margin on ICSR processing services expands materially without volume growth. For a CRO billing time-and-materials or per-case pricing, this translates directly to better unit economics.

Mechanism 2

Volume scaling without proportional headcount

The primary constraint on PV CRO revenue growth is headcount: more clients and more cases require more PV scientists, QPPV-qualified staff, and medical reviewers. AI-augmented workflows change this ratio. A team that can process 30% more cases with the same headcount (through AI triage, auto-coding, and automated submission tracking) supports revenue growth without proportional cost growth. This is the structural lever for scaling from current revenue to €200M by 2030.

Mechanism 3

Market position and client acquisition

As GVP 2026 makes AI-capable PV systems an expectation, clients across the EU and UK will increasingly require AI-integrated services as a contract condition. Biomapas's current positioning — as a high-quality European CRO with strong QPPV network coverage across the EU, UK, and Turkey — is defensible today. In 2–3 years, without a demonstrable AI capability, it will be increasingly difficult to win competitive bids against IQVIA, Parexel, and ArisGlobal-powered competitors. The strategic question is not whether to invest in AI — it is whether to invest early enough to capture the market positioning premium or late enough to be in catch-up mode.
